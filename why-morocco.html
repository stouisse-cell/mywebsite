Six Reasons

Why Kénitra Works
for International Investors

Kénitra is not just a Moroccan city story. It is a city at the intersection of every major growth driver in the country — industrial, infrastructural, and demographic — at a price point that still makes sense.

⚖️
Full Foreign Ownership

Foreign nationals can own residential property outright — freehold, under their own name, with no partner or special structure required. Title is notarised and registered with the national Land Registry (ANCFCC).

💱
Free Repatriation of Capital

Purchase financed in foreign currency through a convertible dirham account? Morocco's Office des Changes guarantees your right to transfer rental income and sale proceeds back abroad — in full. This protection applies to all Kénitra investments made through a convertible dirham account.

📈
Strong & Stable Growth

GDP growth of 3.2% in 2024, projected at 3.9% in 2025 and 3.7% in 2026 (IMF). Inflation at 0.9% in 2024. One of Africa's highest sovereign credit ratings. A macro environment that protects the value of property assets.

✈️
World Cup 2030 Infrastructure

Morocco co-hosts the FIFA World Cup with Spain and Portugal in 2030. Rabat — the host city 30 minutes from Kénitra by TGV — is receiving billions in upgrades. Kénitra is on the corridor, and property values are already responding.

🌍
Gateway Location

Two hours from Madrid. Four hours from Paris. Kénitra sits on Morocco's Atlantic corridor, with its own TGV station and direct motorway connections. As Morocco becomes the logistics hub for three continents, Kénitra is at the centre of that story.

💰
Competitive Entry Prices

The national average price per square metre is around 10,500 MAD — roughly €980/m². Premium apartments in cities like Kénitra and Rabat represent a fraction of the cost of comparable Mediterranean markets in Spain, Portugal, or France.

The City

Why Kénitra
Is Different

Kénitra means "little bridge" in Arabic. It sits between Casablanca and Tangier — Morocco's two biggest economic engines — connected by TGV, motorway, and a growing industrial corridor that is drawing billions in global investment. This is a city that has not yet been priced for what it is becoming.

01
Stellantis — A €1.2 Billion Vote of Confidence

In July 2025, Stellantis inaugurated a €1.2 billion expansion of its Kénitra manufacturing plant — one of the largest industrial investments in Morocco's history. The expanded facility targets 535,000 vehicles per year and creates over 3,100 direct jobs. When one of the world's largest automotive groups doubles down on a city, the real estate market follows.

€1.2B
Stellantis expansion investment, 2025
02
TGV Connected — 30 Minutes from Rabat

Kénitra is served by Morocco's high-speed rail network, placing it 30 minutes from the capital Rabat and under two hours from Casablanca. The city's modern TGV station — an architectural landmark in its own right — signals the level of infrastructure investment the government has committed to this region.

30 min
To Rabat by TGV
03
Atlantic Free Zone — Industrial Engine

The Atlantic Free Zone in Kénitra is home to over 30 factories and 20,000 workers, with Stellantis as its anchor tenant. The zone is attracting suppliers, logistics operators, and technology companies from across Europe and Asia — each bringing a workforce that needs housing, services, and quality urban infrastructure.

20,000+
Workers in the Atlantic Free Zone
04
Affordable — Before the Market Catches Up

Premium apartments in Kénitra are priced at around 10,000–12,000 MAD/m² — significantly below Rabat and Casablanca, and at a fraction of comparable properties in southern Europe. The fundamentals driving price growth are already in place. The market has not yet caught up with the city's trajectory. That gap is the opportunity.

05
World Cup 2030 — Regional Impact

While Kénitra is not itself a World Cup host city, its proximity to Rabat — which is a host city — and its position on Morocco's main TGV corridor means it will benefit directly from the infrastructure upgrades, increased tourism, and international attention that the tournament brings to the entire Rabat-Salé-Kénitra region.

06
Young, Growing Population

Kénitra's population is young, growing, and increasingly employed in the industrial and services sectors. The city's expanding workforce — drawn by the Atlantic Free Zone and related industries — creates sustained structural demand for quality housing. This is not speculative demand. It is demographic and economic reality.
